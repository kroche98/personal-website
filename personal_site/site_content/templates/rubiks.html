Rubik's Cube

Rules

The Rubik's Cube is a combination puzzle which was invented in 1974 by Ernő Rubik. Since its invention, more than 400 million cubes have been sold. The goal is to scramble the cube and then restore it to its original state where each face is a single solid color.

The cube has inspired numerous variants. Here you can interact with the original 3×3×3 cube, a 2×2×2 cube, or a 3×3×3 “Cubix Tube.”

The moves corresponding to 90º clockwise rotations are denoted by L, R, F, B, U, and D. The inverse moves (corresponding to 90º counterclockwise rotations) are denoted by Li, Ri, Fi, Bi, Ui, and Di. The moves corresponding to 180º rotations are denoted by L2, R2, F2, B2, U2, and D2.

The maximum number of moves required to solve the regular 3×3×3 cube (often called “God’s number”) is 20, as was proved in 2010 by Tomas Rokicki, Herbert Kociemba, et al. For the 2×2×2 cube, God’s number is 11. This number is small enough that I was able to write a meet-in-the-middle solution algorithm that runs in your browser and can always solve the 2×2×2 cube optimally. For the 3×3×3 cube, the size of the search space is too large, and a solver using the same strategy would run out of memory.

The cube has a deep mathematical structure related to group theory. To see my senior thesis which explores these topics and describes the “Cubix Tube” variant, click here.
